True Size of the Universe

Three attempts at setting the record straight

About This Project

True Size of the Universe is a design-led research project investigating how visual representations of the solar system shape our perception of cosmic scale. From educational posters to museum exhibits, the way we illustrate space is riddled with distortions: planets are shown far too close together, their sizes wildly exaggerated, and the vastness of interplanetary space almost entirely erased.

These visual shortcuts are often driven by practical constraints — page sizes, screen widths, or the limits of human attention. But over time, they accumulate into a shared visual language that profoundly misrepresents the truth. We grow up with an image of the solar system that is neat, compact, and wildly inaccurate. The result is not just a warped understanding of space, but a diminished sense of the vastness and fragility of our place within it.

True Size of the Universe responds to this problem through three interconnected visual experiments that aim to make invisible scales visible:

Scroll

A data-driven website that invites users to scroll across the solar system at true scale. What begins as a familiar web interaction slowly turns uncanny: planets shrink to small, nearly invisible dots; kilometers of empty space pass beneath the user’s cursor; and planetary encounters become rare events. By preserving accurate proportions and distances, this experiment transforms a simple act of scrolling into a contemplative, time-based journey across astronomical space. It challenges the viewer’s expectations of digital interfaces and reminds us how vast and sparse the solar system truly is.

AR Walk

An augmented reality experience that overlays the solar system onto real-world locations using mobile devices. Each planet is placed at its correct size and distance from the Sun, mapped across a walking route that stretches over a kilometer. Unlike static diagrams, this spatial installation turns the viewer into a participant: you must walk the actual distance between planets, revealing how far apart they truly are — and how little of that space we typically see. By stepping through the solar system at human scale, AR Walk offers a grounded, embodied perspective on a cosmic reality we usually experience only in abstraction.

Planetarium

A modern, interactive take on the traditional planetarium. This simulation presents the solar system in motion, with planets orbiting the Sun at accurate relative speeds and trajectories. It corrects the common misconception that planets move in perfect sync or rest on a single orbital plane. Users can speed up or slow down time, rotate the system, and observe how motion and distance play out dynamically over time. This experiment is not just about scale, but about rhythm — revealing how celestial mechanics unfold continuously, in ways that static images can never convey.

By combining design, code, and critical storytelling, this project reframes the solar system not as an abstract diagram, but as a vast spatial structure — one that can only be understood through experience, distortion-aware design, and a healthy sense of humility. It challenges viewers to reconsider the familiar diagrams we've come to accept, and to imagine new ways of seeing the cosmos more truthfully.

About the Designer

I'm Nore Kerkhofs, a design student with a deep interest in how visual systems shape the way we understand the world. My work often explores the overlap between science, data, and storytelling — especially in areas where complex information is simplified or misrepresented.

With True Size of the Universe, I wanted to challenge the familiar diagrams I grew up seeing in schoolbooks and museums. Why are the planets always shown in a neat row? Why is the space between them so compressed? This project became a way for me to explore those questions, not just through research, but by building tools and experiences that offer alternative ways of seeing.

I use design not just to communicate, but to investigate. For me, this project is as much about critical thinking as it is about aesthetics — and about making space feel as strange, vast, and beautiful as it really is.
